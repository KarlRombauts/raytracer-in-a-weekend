The Jacobian is a stretch factor

When a mapping stretches space, densities change. That’s the whole idea.

You flagged the right gap. “Jacobian” sounds like heavy calculus, but the piece you need is one sentence: a Jacobian is how much a mapping stretches or squishes area near a point — and densities react to that stretch. No matrices required for the intuition. Let’s build it up.

1D first: a derivative is a stretch factor

Take y = f(x). Step a tiny dx and you move dy = f′(x)·dx. So f′(x) is the local stretch factor: output-length per input-length. If f′ = 3 near some x, that spot gets stretched 3×.

Now add density — the key move

A density is “stuff per length” (or per area): dots per cm, probability per unit, radiance per steradian. Picture a strip of rubber with dots painted at even spacing. Stretch a section to 3× its length: the same dots now cover 3× the space, so the dots-per-length there drops to ⅓.

The rule you need Under a mapping, density divides by the stretch factor. Stretch a region (bigger) → density falls. Squish it (smaller) → density concentrates, density rises. In 2D the “stretch factor” is the area stretch — and that number is the Jacobian.

2D: the Jacobian is the area stretch

A mapping from a 2D input (like your image’s (u, v)) to a 2D output (like directions on the sphere) turns a tiny input square into a tiny output patch — maybe bigger, maybe smaller, maybe sheared. The Jacobian is the ratio of those areas: output area ÷ input area. If it’s 2, patches double; if ⅓, they shrink to a third.

A friendly sanity-check you may have seen: polar coordinates. A little patch at radius r has sides dr and r·dθ, so its area is r·dr·dθ. The Jacobian is r — patches far from the centre are bigger. Same idea, familiar setting.

Your actual mapping: the sky is a stretched map

Here’s why this is your problem, not a detour. The equirectangular image is a flat map of a globe — and flat maps stretch. On a world map, Greenland looks as big as Africa; on the globe it’s tiny. Going image → globe, the poles shrink.

An entire top row of the image — many texels across — all map to nearly the same direction (straight up). So a unit of image area near the pole covers only a sliver of actual sky. That shrink factor — the amount of real solid angle per unit of image area — works out to

Jacobian = 2π²·sin θ

…which goes to zero at the poles (sin 0 = 0) and is largest at the horizon. You don’t need to derive it today — just read it as “the sky-area a texel is worth,” tiny at the poles.

What you can now say To turn a probability density on the flat image into one on the sphere of directions, divide by 2π²·sin θ. That single division is “applying the Jacobian.” Everything else in the sky-pdf is bookkeeping around it.

Where this is heading (Lesson 4)

Next lesson we cash it in: take a sampled (u, v) from Lesson 2, turn it into a world direction, and divide its image-pdf by 2π²·sin θ to get the solid-angle pdf MIS needs. And you’ll see the payoff — the sin θ you multiply in as a weight (Lesson 1) cancels the sin θ you divide by here, leaving you sampling directions in proportion to their brightness. That cancellation is the satisfying click.
