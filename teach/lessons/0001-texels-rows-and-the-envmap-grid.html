Lesson 01 · Env-map MIS

The environment map is a grid

Texels, rows, and what it means to “pick a bright direction.”

Your goal is to make a bright HDR sky light your scene without noise. Today’s single win: stop seeing the sky as a lookup function and start seeing it as a grid of cells you can pick from. Every idea later — pdfs, the sin θ weight, MIS — sits on top of this picture.

A texel is one pixel of the sky image

Texel = “texture element” = one pixel of the HDR. In your code (src/texture/env_map.rs) the whole sky is just a flat array of them:

data: Vec<[f32; 3]> — width × height texels, row-major.

texel(x, y) reads one cell: data[y * width + x]. Each holds a linear-light radiance — and because it’s HDR, a texel’s value can be far above 1.0 (the sun might be 10,000; the sky, 0.5). That huge range is exactly why sampling matters.

A texel is not a light, a ray, or a direction. It’s a stored number — a measurement of how much light arrives from one little patch of sky. Turning texels into directions is the next step.

A row is a ring of sky at one height

The image is equirectangular: x (a column) is the compass direction (longitude), y (a row) is how far up or down you look (latitude). Your sample(dir) makes this precise:

u = 0.5 + atan2(dz, dx) / 2π  → column  ·  v = acos(dir.y) / π  → row.

So row 0 (top) is straight up (+Y), the middle row is the horizon, and the bottom row is straight down. One row = every direction at a fixed height — a horizontal ring around you.

Importance sampling = pick a texel by its brightness

Right now your tracer only finds the sun by accident: it bounces a ray off a diffuse surface in a random direction and hopes it lands on those few bright texels. Almost always it misses → the surface looks too dark on most samples and blindingly bright on the rare hit. That flicker is a firefly.

The core idea Instead of picking a direction and hoping, pick a texel with probability proportional to how bright it is, then aim a ray straight at that texel’s direction. Bright texels (the sun) get chosen often; dark sky rarely. Same picture, far less noise.

Picking a bright cell: row first, then column

How do you pick one cell from a 2D grid, weighted by brightness? You split it into two 1D picks:

1. Pick a row with probability proportional to that row’s total brightness. (A row containing the sun is very likely.) This is the marginal distribution.
2. Within that row, pick a column by the texels’ brightness. This is the conditional distribution.

Row-then-column, marginal-then-conditional — that pair is what PBRT’s Distribution2D stores and samples. Nothing more mysterious than “which shelf, then which book on it.”

One caveat you’ll need soon: the poles are squished

An equirectangular map stretches the top and bottom rows. A texel near “straight up” represents a tiny pinch of real sky; a texel near the horizon represents a wide swath. So brightness alone isn’t the right weight — a texel’s sampling weight is luminance × sin θ, where θ is its latitude angle (for your layout, row y has θ = π·(y + 0.5)/height). Without the sin θ you’d over-pick the poles.

Don’t worry about deriving sin θ yet — just file it away: “weight = brightness × sin θ.” The why (it’s a change-of-variables Jacobian between the image and the sphere) is Lesson 2, and it’s the same factor that turns your sample into a correct pdf for MIS.
