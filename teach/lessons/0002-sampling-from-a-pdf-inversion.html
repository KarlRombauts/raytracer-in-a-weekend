Lesson 02 · Env-map MIS

How a computer samples from a PDF

You never weight the random number. You reshape the interval it lands in.

You noticed the gap exactly: a generator hands you a uniform number in [0, 1) — every value equally likely — yet you want a bright texel picked far more often than a dark one. Where does the weighting come from? The trick has a name: inverse transform sampling (a.k.a. the inversion method), and once you see it you’ll see it everywhere.

The one idea The pdf never multiplies your random draw. Instead it carves the unit interval [0, 1) into segments whose widths are the probabilities. Then you drop your uniform draw on that interval and report whichever segment it landed in. Uniform draw + uneven carving = non-uniform samples.

The discrete recipe — the one you’ll actually code

Your envmap is a finite set of cells (rows, or texels within a row), each with a weight wᵢ = its brightness. You want cell i chosen with probability pᵢ = wᵢ / Σw. Three steps:

1. Normalise & accumulate. Build a running cumulative sum C: it starts at 0 and climbs to 1, adding pᵢ at each step. That array of cut-points is the CDF (cumulative distribution function). A bright cell adds a big jump.
2. Draw one uniform u ∈ [0, 1).
3. Find the segment. Return the first cell whose cut-point exceeds u — the smallest i with C[i] > u. Because C is sorted, that’s a binary search: O(log N), even for a million texels.

A worked example

Four cells with weights w = [1, 6, 1, 2] (total 10), so p = [0.1, 0.6, 0.1, 0.2]. The cumulative cut-points:

Draw u = 0.43 → it’s in [0.1, 0.7) → cell 1. Draw u = 0.95 → [0.8, 1.0) → cell 3. The generator stayed perfectly uniform; the carving did the weighting. Run it a million times and cell 1 shows up ~600,000 times — exactly its weight.

Don’t forget the pdf itself. The sampler must also report how likely its pick was — pᵢ = wᵢ / Σw — because MIS needs it later. It’s right there in the same array. Sampling and “what was its pdf?” are two sides of one structure.

Why it works (30-second version)

The CDF F is a ramp from 0 to 1. Steep where density is high, flat where it’s low. Sampling is reading the ramp backwards: pick a height u uniformly on the vertical axis, slide across to the ramp, drop to the outcome. A steep step (high probability) spans a tall band of u-values, so it catches your draw often; a flat stretch catches it rarely. That’s the whole mechanism — x = F⁻¹(u).

Two flavours of “invert the CDF”

Analytic — when the pdf has a clean formula you can invert with algebra. You already have one: cosine_direction_from_uv in integrator::common turns two uniform numbers into a cosine-weighted bounce. That’s inverse-transform sampling in closed form (Malley’s method).
Tabulated — when the “pdf” is just measured data with no formula (your HDR sky), you can’t invert with algebra, so you build the cumulative array and binary-search it. That’s the envmap case — and it’s the only new machinery you need for Lesson 1’s “row, then column.” The row pick is one tabulated inversion over rows; the column pick is another over the chosen row.
